I am writing to submit our manuscript entitled "The Severity-Sex Gradient in Adverse Drug Reactions: Fatal Events Approach Parity While Mild Reactions Show 64% Female Predominance" for consideration as an Original Article in Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics.

This study reports a novel quantitative relationship between adverse drug reaction severity and sex distribution, derived from systematic analysis of 14.5 million FAERS reports. We demonstrate that fatal and life-threatening adverse events approach sex parity in reporting, while non-serious and mild events exhibit progressively stronger female predominance, reaching 64% for the least severe category. This severity-sex gradient is highly significant (Spearman rho = 0.93, p = 0.003) and robust across therapeutic classes, anatomical systems, and reporting periods.

The clinical implications are substantial. Current pharmacovigilance practice implicitly weights serious and fatal outcomes most heavily in signal detection. Our findings suggest this approach may systematically obscure the largest sex differences in drug safety, which concentrate in the non-serious events that constitute the majority of the reporting burden. We propose that sex-stratified signal detection, particularly for non-serious outcomes, should become standard practice in post-marketing surveillance.

This work aligns closely with the scope and readership of Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics. The recent FAERS methodological guide by Potter et al. (2025) published in your journal underscored the importance of rigorous analytical frameworks for spontaneous reporting data -- our severity-stratified approach complements and extends those recommendations. Furthermore, the ASCPT community's longstanding emphasis on translating pharmacological insights into clinical practice makes CPT the ideal venue for a finding that has direct, actionable implications for how pharmacovigilance signals are prioritized and investigated.

The manuscript is approximately 5,000 words with 5 figures and 2 supplementary tables. All underlying data and analytical code are publicly available via GitHub and Zenodo, supporting full reproducibility. The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere.
