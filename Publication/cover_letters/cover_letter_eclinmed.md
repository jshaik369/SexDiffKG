I am writing to submit our manuscript entitled "SexDiffKG: A Sex-Stratified Drug Safety Knowledge Graph Reveals Systematic Sex Differences in Adverse Drug Reactions" for consideration as an Original Research article in eClinicalMedicine.

This work presents SexDiffKG, the first comprehensive sex-stratified drug safety knowledge graph, constructed from 14.5 million FAERS reports spanning two decades and integrating pharmacological, genomic, and clinical data into a unified graph of 109,867 nodes and 1.8 million edges. Through systematic disproportionality analysis, we identified 96,281 statistically significant sex-differential adverse drug reaction signals and validated our findings against 40 established literature benchmarks, achieving 82.8% concordance.

Our study reports two previously undescribed phenomena with direct clinical implications. First, the severity-sex gradient (Spearman rho = 0.93, p = 0.003): fatal adverse reactions approach sex parity, while mild reactions exhibit a striking 64% female predominance, suggesting that current pharmacovigilance systems may systematically underweight the burden of non-serious adverse events in women. Second, the anti-regression phenomenon (rho = 1.0, p = 6.6e-64): contrary to regression-to-the-mean expectations, female reporting bias intensifies monotonically with increasing sample size, indicating that sex differences in drug safety are structural rather than artifactual.

I believe this work is particularly well-suited for eClinicalMedicine for several reasons. Your journal published the landmark study by Watson et al. (2019), which demonstrated sex disparities in global ADR reporting using VigiBase data across half a century. Our work extends that foundational analysis by constructing a structured knowledge graph that enables mechanistic interrogation of sex differences, quantifying the severity-sex gradient that Watson et al. noted but did not formally characterize, and providing open computational infrastructure for reproducible sex-stratified pharmacovigilance research. Additionally, the accompanying editorial by Zucker and Prendergast (2019) in your journal called for systematic sex-specific analysis in drug safety -- SexDiffKG directly answers that call with scalable, open-source tooling.

The manuscript is approximately 5,000 words with 6 figures and 3 supplementary tables. All data and code are publicly available on GitHub and Zenodo, in keeping with eClinicalMedicine's commitment to open science and reproducibility.

The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere. I have no conflicts of interest to declare. I am the sole author of this work.
